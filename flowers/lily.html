The Meaning of Lilies

Graceful, fragrant, and deeply symbolic, lilies have captivated hearts and inspired cultures for thousands of years. Discover why these elegant blooms remain one of the most beloved flowers in the world.

A Flower with Ancient Roots

The lily is one of the oldest cultivated flowers on Earth. Depictions of lilies appear in artwork dating back nearly 3,000 years, found in ancient Minoan frescoes on the island of Crete. The ancient Greeks and Romans held lilies in high esteem, weaving them into their mythologies and daily rituals. In ancient Egypt, the lily was a symbol of Upper Egypt and frequently appeared in hieroglyphic writing and temple decorations. Throughout the centuries, the lily has maintained its status as a flower of profound significance, gracing royal gardens, religious altars, and bridal bouquets alike.

The word "lily" is derived from the Latin lilium, which itself traces back to the Greek leirion. Across nearly every language and civilization, the lily has been associated with beauty, grace, and the divine. Its tall, upright stems and trumpet-shaped blooms give it a regal bearing that few other flowers can match, making it a natural choice for ceremonies and celebrations of all kinds.

Popular Types of Lilies

The lily family is remarkably diverse, encompassing hundreds of species and thousands of hybrid cultivars. Each type carries its own personality, fragrance, and visual appeal. Understanding the differences can help you choose the perfect lily for any occasion.

Stargazer Lily

Perhaps the most recognizable of all lily varieties, the stargazer lily is known for its large, upward-facing blooms splashed with vivid pink and speckled with deep crimson spots. Created in 1978 by hybridizer Leslie Woodriff, the stargazer was named for its unique habit of facing the sky rather than nodding downward like many other lilies. Its intoxicating fragrance fills entire rooms and makes it a favorite in floral arrangements. The stargazer symbolizes ambition, prosperity, and encouragement, making it an ideal gift for someone embarking on a new chapter in life.

Calla Lily

Despite its name, the calla lily is not actually a true lily but belongs to the genus Zantedeschia. Regardless of its botanical classification, the calla lily has earned its place among the most elegant flowers in existence. Its smooth, sculptural trumpet shape and waxy petals give it an air of modern sophistication. Calla lilies are particularly popular in wedding bouquets and contemporary floral design. They symbolize magnificence, beauty, and purity, and are available in a stunning range of colors from classic white to deep burgundy, sunny yellow, and even near-black.

Tiger Lily

Bold, fiery, and impossible to ignore, the tiger lily features dramatic recurved petals covered in dark spots that resemble the markings of its namesake animal. Native to eastern Asia, the tiger lily has been cultivated for centuries, prized both for its ornamental beauty and its edible bulbs, which are a staple in traditional Asian cuisine. In the language of flowers, tiger lilies represent wealth, pride, and confidence. They are the perfect choice for someone with a vibrant personality who lives life boldly and unapologetically.

Easter Lily

The Easter lily is the quintessential symbol of spring renewal and Christian celebration. Its pure white, trumpet-shaped blooms and heavenly fragrance have made it the iconic flower of the Easter season in many Western countries. Native to the Ryukyu Islands of Japan, the Easter lily was introduced to the Western world in the late nineteenth century and quickly became associated with themes of hope, resurrection, and new beginnings. Churches across the globe fill their sanctuaries with Easter lilies during the holiday, creating an atmosphere of reverence and joy.

Asiatic Lily

Asiatic lilies are among the most popular garden varieties due to their hardiness, early blooming season, and astonishing color range. Available in nearly every shade imaginable, from soft pastels to electric oranges and deep reds, Asiatic lilies are remarkably easy to grow and make excellent cut flowers. While they lack the strong fragrance of their oriental cousins, their vibrant appearance more than compensates. They represent cheerfulness, energy, and the simple pleasures of a sunny garden day.

The Language of Lily Colors

Like many flowers, lilies communicate different emotions and sentiments depending on their color. Understanding these meanings allows you to craft a truly thoughtful floral message.

  • White Lilies: Purity, virtue, and innocence. White lilies are the most traditional choice for weddings, baptisms, and sympathy arrangements. They convey a sense of reverence and spiritual devotion that transcends cultural boundaries.
  • Pink Lilies: Prosperity, abundance, and admiration. Pink lilies are a wonderful way to express good wishes and appreciation. They carry a gentle warmth that makes them suitable for congratulations, thank-you gestures, and expressions of friendship.
  • Orange Lilies: Confidence, energy, and passion. Orange lilies radiate enthusiasm and positive energy. They are an excellent choice for celebrating achievements, encouraging someone through a challenge, or adding a burst of warmth to any arrangement.
  • Yellow Lilies: Joy, gratitude, and good health. Yellow lilies brighten any space and any mood. They are perfect for get-well wishes, cheerful greetings, and moments when you want to spread sunshine.
  • Red Lilies: Love, desire, and romantic passion. While roses often steal the spotlight in matters of the heart, red lilies offer an equally powerful declaration of deep love and longing.

Lilies in Culture and Mythology

Few flowers are as deeply embedded in cultural and religious traditions as the lily. Its symbolism spans continents and millennia, appearing in sacred texts, classical art, and folk traditions around the world.

Lilies in Christianity

In Christian tradition, the lily holds a place of extraordinary reverence. The white lily, often called the Madonna Lily, is closely associated with the Virgin Mary and represents her purity, grace, and divine motherhood. Renaissance painters frequently depicted the Archangel Gabriel presenting a white lily to Mary during the Annunciation, cementing the flower as an enduring symbol of sacred innocence. To this day, white lilies appear in churches during Easter and other holy celebrations, serving as a visual reminder of resurrection and eternal hope.

Lilies in Greek Mythology

According to Greek mythology, lilies sprang from the milk of Hera, queen of the gods. The story tells that Zeus wished his son Heracles to be nursed by Hera while she slept. When Hera awoke and pushed the infant away, drops of her milk fell to the earth and blossomed into lilies, while the drops that flew upward became the Milky Way. This origin story gave the lily its association with motherhood, fertility, and celestial beauty. The ancient Greeks also used lilies in wedding garlands, believing the flower would bless the union with abundance and harmony.

Lilies in Chinese Culture

In China, lilies are traditionally given at weddings because the Chinese word for lily, baihe, sounds similar to the phrase meaning "together for a hundred years." The flower symbolizes a harmonious and enduring marriage, making it one of the most popular wedding flowers in Chinese culture. Lilies also represent good luck, and they are commonly included in New Year celebrations and festive gatherings.

Lilies in French History

The fleur-de-lis, a stylized lily design, has been a symbol of French royalty and heraldry for centuries. While its exact origins are debated, the fleur-de-lis appeared on the coat of arms of French kings from the twelfth century onward and came to represent sovereignty, perfection, and divine right. Today, it remains one of the most recognizable heraldic symbols in the world, adorning flags, architecture, and cultural emblems far beyond the borders of France.

When to Send Lilies

The versatility of lilies makes them appropriate for nearly any occasion, but some moments call for them especially. Here are some perfect times to share these graceful blooms:

  • Weddings and Engagements: White and blush lilies add timeless elegance to any celebration of love. Their stately beauty and romantic fragrance create an unforgettable atmosphere.
  • Sympathy and Remembrance: White lilies are among the most common choices for funeral flowers, offering a message of peace, restored innocence, and the hope of renewal beyond loss.
  • Mother's Day: Given the lily's deep connection to motherhood through both Christian and Greek tradition, lilies are a meaningful and beautiful choice for honoring mothers and maternal figures.
  • New Beginnings: Whether someone is starting a new job, moving to a new home, or welcoming a baby, lilies represent fresh starts and optimistic futures.
  • Congratulations: Stargazer and oriental lilies in vibrant colors are perfect for celebrating promotions, graduations, and personal achievements.
  • Just Because: Sometimes the most meaningful gestures come without a reason. A bouquet of lilies sent on an ordinary day can turn it into an extraordinary one.

Fun Facts About Lilies

  • There are roughly 100 true species in the genus Lilium, with thousands of hybrid cultivars developed by breeders worldwide.
  • Lily bulbs have been used as a food source for centuries. In parts of Asia, dried lily bulbs are a common ingredient in soups and traditional dishes.
  • Lilies are extremely toxic to cats. Even small amounts of pollen, petals, or water from a lily vase can cause severe kidney failure in felines, so cat owners should exercise great caution.
  • The oldest known depictions of lilies come from a Minoan fresco on the island of Crete, dating back to approximately 1580 BCE.
  • Some lily varieties can grow up to six feet tall, making them among the tallest garden perennials.
  • The fragrance of oriental lilies is so powerful that a single stem can perfume an entire room for days.
  • In Victorian England, receiving lilies carried a coded message. White lilies meant purity, while orange lilies could signal hatred or disdain, depending on the context.
  • The lily is the birth flower for the month of May, making it an especially thoughtful gift for anyone born during that month.

Lilies in the Modern World

Today, lilies remain one of the most commercially important cut flowers in the global floral industry. The Netherlands leads the world in lily bulb production, exporting billions of bulbs each year to growers and florists on every continent. Advances in hybridization have produced lilies in colors, sizes, and forms that would have astonished gardeners of previous centuries, including double-flowered varieties, dwarf cultivars suitable for containers, and pollen-free hybrids that eliminate the risk of staining clothes and furniture.

Beyond the garden and the vase, lilies continue to inspire artists, perfumers, and designers. The lily's elegant silhouette appears in textiles, jewelry, tattoo art, and interior design. In perfumery, lily-of-the-valley and lily absolute are prized ingredients that lend a fresh, green, and softly floral character to some of the world's most celebrated fragrances.

Whether you are drawn to the stately beauty of the Easter lily, the bold drama of the stargazer, or the sculptural elegance of the calla, there is a lily for every taste, every occasion, and every emotion. These timeless blooms have graced human civilization for millennia, and their enduring appeal shows no sign of fading.
